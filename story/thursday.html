He tilted his head to read RS100 on the tyre before getting on the bus. People waiting for buses looked very busy. Their intent gazes didn’t shorten the wait. Frowns and anxiety.

He showed his ID to the driver. He was glad that he didn’t have to struggle like those that stood waiting. He sat on a window seat and put on his headphones, music was louder than the sunshine.

He felt something at his feet. Something pulling his shoe laces. For an instant it felt as if a lace go stuck somewhere. He looked down two questions struck him at a time. First, why is one lace longer than the other? Second, what is a pup doing there?

He beamed and hung his bag on a broken handle behind the seat. Gently, he picked the pup and placed it on his lap. The lady sitting next bent away. She suppressed a shout and asked to put it away. He never thought someone could be afraid of a little dog. When the lady insisted, he said, rather loudly, “You want me to throw it out of the window?” Embarrassed, he took off his headphones while the lady looked away.

Traffic was heavy as usual. There was a truck standing next. Three boys were sitting in the truck bed eating biscuits. He opened the window and shouted, “Biscuits !”. Then he raised the puppy and took out some money from his pocket. One boy stepped on the tailgate and gave him a packet of biscuit. In return, he gave money.

The lady watched and conveyed disgust as the puppy licked biscuit crumbs off his hand. He got up and stood in the aisle, holding the puppy. Then he wrapped one arm around a handrail. Everyone was looking at him. He had never seen so many people smiling in the bus. That too on their way to the office. Moreover, never had so many people smiled at him. He had their attention. In fact, it had their attention. Many stroked the puppy, taking turns, without saying a word. Some started talking about their pets. Usually, few people talk to each other in the bus. The driver looked through the rear view mirror. He saw the driver also had a smile. The center of attraction was fed some biscuits, this time dipped in water.

He looked through the large windows. He noticed new buildings, lots of giant billboards. He caught spelling mistakes. An all time favorite. It reminded him of the summer at grandma’s house. His father misspelled “business”. On being corrected he disagreed. Without Internet or even a dictionary, it was hard to prove. After a couple of days, his father admitted his mistake. They both felt happy and proud. Doing something right makes you happy. Today, his legs won’t give up, those arms know no pain. He didn’t even dislike the lady anymore.

Before they reached the bus bay, he got down. He placed the puppy and biscuits on a sidewalk. He walked toward the bus bay. As soon as he reached the floor, he went to the restroom and washed his hands. He washed repeatedly and vigorously. His fingers could feel the puppy’s forehead. At each stroke, it would close its eyes. When he would stop, it looked up.

It was one lousy soap.
